Tour Overview

This walking tour follows the footsteps of Charles Dickens (1812-1870) through Victorian London, visiting locations that shaped his life and inspired his greatest novels. The route spans approximately 4 miles and takes 5-7 hours including stops.

Important: Warren's Blacking Factory was not located on Villiers Street. The factory operated at two locations: Hungerford Stairs (beneath Charing Cross Station) and 6 Chandos Place, Covent Garden.

SECTION 1: The Strand & Covent Garden

1Villiers Street & Warren's Blacking Factory Sites

Address: 43 Villiers Street, London WC2N 6NE
Nearest tube: Embankment, Charing Cross

What You'll See: A building called "Kipling House" with a blue plaque - but this has no Dickens connection. Rudyard Kipling lived here (1889-1891).

The Real Warren's Blacking Factory Locations:

First Location - Hungerford Stairs (1823-1824): Beneath Charing Cross Station. The original factory site lies buried beneath the railway bridge, demolished in the 1860s. At age 11, Charles was sent to work here while his father was in Marshalsea Prison.

Second Location - Chandos Place (January-September 1824): 6 Chandos Place (corner with Bedford Street), WC2N 4HG. A blue plaque marks where TGI Friday's now stands. Young Dickens worked in a window visible to crowds, adding public humiliation to his misery.

2Royal Society of Arts

Address: 8 John Adam Street, London WC2N 6EZ
Walking time: 3 minutes from Chandos Place
Opening hours: Monday-Friday 8am-8pm; Coffee House 8:30am-5pm

Dickens Connection: Charles Dickens was a Fellow of the RSA, serving as Vice President and on committees. The building incorporates 18 Adam Street, originally the Adelphi Tavern, mentioned in The Pickwick Papers.

Architectural Gem: Designed by Robert Adam (1774), featuring original ceilings, 14 fireplaces, and the Great Room with James Barry's epic painting series.

3Rules Restaurant

Address: 35 Maiden Lane, Covent Garden, London WC2E 7LB
Walking time: 4 minutes
Phone: 020 7836 5314

Dickens Connection: Dickens was a regular patron of Rules, London's oldest restaurant (founded 1798). The Victorian atmosphere he knew is preserved in the dark wood paneling and red velvet.

4Bow Street

Location: Between Russell Street and Long Acre, WC2E
Walking time: 2 minutes

Context: Home of the Bow Street Runners (Britain's first police force, c.1750) and the Magistrates' Court (1740-2006). Represents the Victorian justice system Dickens critiqued.

5Dickens' Magazine Offices

Address: 26 Wellington Street, Covent Garden, WC2E 7DD
Walking time: 1 minute

Critical Site: Housed the offices of All The Year Round (1859-1870) and Dickens's private apartments after his separation. He worked and lived here during his final decade. A commemorative plaque marks the building.

SECTION 2: Holborn & Legal London

6The Old Curiosity Shop

Address: 13-14 Portsmouth Street, Holborn, WC2A 2ES
Walking time: 12 minutes

Important Note: Despite the inscription, this is NOT the shop that inspired the novel. Built c.1567, it's one of London's oldest timber-framed buildings and survived the Great Fire of 1666.

7Sir John Soane's Museum

Address: 13 Lincoln's Inn Fields, Holborn, WC2A 2ES
Walking time: 3 minutes
Hours: Wednesday-Sunday 10am-5pm; FREE entry

Adjacent to Lincoln's Inn where Dickens's friend John Forster lived. An extraordinary museum preserved as at the architect's death in 1837.

8aLincoln's Inn

Entrance: Chancery Lane or Newman's Row, WC2A 3QA
Walking time: 2 minutes (adjacent)

Dickens Connection: Central to Bleak House (1852-53). The Old Hall housed the Court of Chancery that opens the novel with its famous "fog" scene. The fictional "Jarndyce and Jarndyce" case exposed legal corruption.

8bGray's Inn

Entrance: Lady Hale Gate, High Holborn, WC1X 8JB
Walking time: 8 minutes

Personal Connection: 15-year-old Dickens worked as a junior clerk at 1 South Square, Gray's Inn (1827-1828), earning 13-15 shillings per week.

9Royal Courts of Justice & The Seven Stars

Royal Courts: Strand, London WC2A 2LL
Seven Stars Pub: 53 Carey Street, WC2A 2JB
Walking time: 7 minutes from Gray's Inn

The Royal Courts (1882) represent the legal reform Dickens championed. The Seven Stars (1602) is the historic pub associated with the courts.

SECTION 3: Fleet Street - Journalism & Literary London

10St Dunstan-in-the-West

Address: 186A Fleet Street, London EC4A 2HR
Walking time: 8 minutes
Hours: Monday-Friday 10am-3pm

The famous clock (1671) with striking giants features in multiple Dickens novels including Barnaby Rudge, David Copperfield, and Master Humphrey's Clock.

11Ye Olde Cheshire Cheese

Address: 145 Fleet Street (entrance via Wine Office Court), EC4A 2BU
Walking time: 3 minutes
Hours: Mon-Fri 11am-11pm; Sat noon-11pm; Sun noon-4pm

Dickens was a regular patron. The pub appears in A Tale of Two Cities. Dating from 1538, rebuilt 1667 after the Great Fire. Features sawdust floors and labyrinthine layout.

12Dr. Johnson's House

Address: 17 Gough Square, London EC4A 3DE
Walking time: 4 minutes
Hours: Tuesday-Saturday 11am-5pm
Admission: £9 adults

Samuel Johnson (1709-1784) compiled his Dictionary here. Both men worked in Fleet Street, Johnson a century before Dickens. Grade I listed Queen Anne townhouse.

13Took's Court - "Cook's Court"

Address: 15 Took's Court, London EC4A 1LB
Walking time: 6 minutes

In Bleak House, Took's Court becomes "Cook's Court" - home to law stationer Mr. Snagsby. Narrow cul-de-sac dating from c.1720.

SECTION 4: Holborn to Clerkenwell

14Staple Inn

Address: High Holborn (south side), London WC1V 7PZ
Walking time: 5 minutes

Featured in The Mystery of Edwin Drood (1870), Dickens's final novel. Built 1585, the last surviving Inn of Chancery. Tudor timber-framed building with distinctive black-and-white façade.

15Furnival's Inn Site

Current Address: Holborn Bars (Prudential Building), 138-142 Holborn
Walking time: 2 minutes

Critical Site: Completely demolished in 1897. Dickens's first independent home (1834-1837) where he wrote The Pickwick Papers. Blue plaque commemorates the site.

SECTION 5: Southwark - Dickens's Childhood Trauma

17Marshalsea Prison Site

Location: Angel Place (off Borough High Street), SE1
Nearest tube: Borough, London Bridge
Access: FREE

The Most Important Dickens Location in London: On February 20, 1824, John Dickens was imprisoned here for a £40 debt. 12-year-old Charles was sent to work at Warren's Blacking Factory. The original southern boundary wall still stands in Angel Place.

Literary Impact: Central to Little Dorrit (1855-1857), where Amy Dorrit is born in the prison. Also influenced David Copperfield and The Pickwick Papers.

18The George Inn

Address: 77 Borough High Street, Southwark, SE1 1NH
Walking time: 3 minutes
Phone: 020 7407 2056

London's Only Surviving Galleried Coaching Inn: Grade I listed, owned by National Trust. Dating from 1677, features distinctive wooden galleries. Referenced in Little Dorrit and Our Mutual Friend.

ENDBorough Market

Address: 8 Southwark Street, London SE1 1TL
Walking time: 2 minutes
Hours: Tue-Fri 10am-5pm; Sat 9am-5pm; Sun 10am-4pm; Closed Monday

1,000 Years of History: Dating back to 1014, London's oldest food market. At its peak during Dickens's lifetime. Victorian glass and iron structures from 1851 still visible. Described in Little Dorrit.

Practical Information

Total Distance: Approximately 4 miles (6.4 km)
Total Walking Time: 1.5-2 hours (excluding stops)
Recommended Tour Time: 5-7 hours including visits
Difficulty: Easy to moderate

Best Days to Visit

  • Tuesday-Friday: Ideal - all sites accessible, less crowded
  • Saturday: Borough Market at its liveliest but most crowded
  • Avoid: Monday (Borough Market closed)

Essential Items

  • Comfortable walking shoes
  • Camera for plaques and architecture
  • Cash and card
  • Water bottle
  • Oyster card for tube

Estimated Costs

Most locations are free. Paid admission: Dr. Johnson's House (£9). Total estimated cost including one meal: £20-50 per person.
